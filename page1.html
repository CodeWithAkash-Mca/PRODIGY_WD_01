The Whispering Attic

October 26, 2023 | By Elias Thorne

It started with a faint rustling, a sound barely audible above the nightly storm. My wife, Sarah, dismissed it as wind. "Just the old house groaning, dear," she'd murmur, pulling the covers tighter. But I knew better. This house, inherited from my eccentric great-aunt Elara, didn't groan. It *whispered*.

The attic was a forbidden zone in Elara's time. Locked, dusty, and shrouded in an unspoken fear. After she passed, leaving us the sprawling, creaking Victorian, I'd found the key – a tarnished silver serpent coiled around a small, iron ring. It felt cold to the touch, even on the warmest days.

The whispers grew. Not words, not exactly. More like the dry crackle of ancient parchment, or the delicate scratching of tiny claws on wood. It was rhythmic, almost a lullaby, but one sung by something utterly alien. Sarah, usually so rational, began to look pale. "Are you sure it's just the pipes?" she'd ask, her eyes darting to the ceiling.

The Ascent

One Tuesday night, a particularly violent tempest raged. The whispers weren't whispers anymore; they were a cacophony, a swirling maelstrom of barely decipherable sounds. I couldn't sleep. Sarah was deep in a restless slumber, muttering something about shadows. I knew I had to go up.

The attic door, when I finally pushed it open, exhaled a breath of stale, cold air. Moonlight, strained through a grimy window, cast long, dancing shadows of forgotten furniture. Dust motes swirled like spectral dancers. And the sound... it was deafening here. It was coming from a large, moth-eaten trunk in the center of the room.

What I Found

My hands trembled as I approached. The trunk wasn't locked. It was almost as if it *wanted* to be opened. Lifting the heavy lid, a gust of frigid air, smelling of decay and old roses, hit me. Inside, nestled among yellowed lace and brittle photographs, was a single, small, leather-bound book.

Its cover was blank, save for a single, intricately carved symbol. As I touched it, the whispering stopped. Complete silence. The kind of silence that screams. The book was cold, colder than the key. And it pulsed. A faint, internal thrum that I felt in my bones.

I didn't open it then. I couldn't. The fear was too great, too overwhelming. I simply closed the trunk, replaced the key, and stumbled back downstairs, leaving the attic door ajar. The whispers haven't returned. But now, every night, I hear a new sound – the faint, rhythmic thrum, coming from my bedside table, where the book now rests. It wants to be opened.
